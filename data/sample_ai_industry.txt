The Rise of Artificial Intelligence Companies

OpenAI was founded in 2015 by Sam Altman, Elon Musk, Greg Brockman, and several other researchers.
The organization was initially structured as a non-profit with a mission to ensure that artificial
general intelligence benefits all of humanity. In 2019, OpenAI transitioned to a "capped-profit"
model to attract the capital needed for large-scale AI research.

Microsoft Corporation became a major strategic partner of OpenAI, investing $1 billion in 2019 and
subsequently leading a multi-billion dollar investment round in 2023, valuing OpenAI at approximately
$29 billion. This partnership gave Microsoft exclusive licensing rights to OpenAI's GPT technology,
which was integrated into Microsoft's Azure cloud platform and the Bing search engine.

Sam Altman serves as CEO of OpenAI and has been a driving force behind the commercialization
of large language models. He previously led Y Combinator, the prestigious Silicon Valley startup
accelerator that has produced companies like Airbnb, Dropbox, and Stripe.

Anthropic is another major AI safety company, founded in 2021 by Dario Amodei and Daniela Amodei,
who previously worked at OpenAI. Anthropic developed Claude, a family of AI assistants designed
with a focus on safety and alignment. The company has received significant investment from
Google and Amazon.

Google DeepMind is Alphabet's AI research division, formed through the merger of Google Brain
and DeepMind in 2023. DeepMind was originally founded in London in 2010 by Demis Hassabis,
Shane Legg, and Mustafa Suleyman, and was acquired by Google in 2014.

Demis Hassabis serves as CEO of Google DeepMind. Under his leadership, DeepMind achieved
landmark results including AlphaGo (defeating the world Go champion in 2016), AlphaFold
(solving the protein folding problem in 2020), and Gemini (Google's flagship LLM family).

The competition between OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google DeepMind has accelerated AI development
significantly. All three organizations focus on large language models, AI safety research,
and the development of multimodal systems capable of processing text, images, audio, and video.

Meta AI, led by Chief AI Scientist Yann LeCun, takes a different philosophical approach,
advocating for open-source AI development. Meta released the LLaMA family of models openly,
enabling researchers and developers worldwide to build applications without API dependency.

Nvidia Corporation has become a critical infrastructure provider for the entire AI industry.
Its H100 and A100 GPU chips are the primary hardware used to train large AI models. CEO Jensen
Huang has positioned Nvidia at the center of the AI supply chain. OpenAI, Anthropic, Google,
and Meta all rely heavily on Nvidia hardware for model training.
